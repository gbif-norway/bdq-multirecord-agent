Quick Wins and What to Look For

The good news is that no changes were automatically made (no "amendments" or "filled-in" values), and no "issues" were flagged in your dataset. This means the tests didn't find any obvious problems that could be automatically fixed or clear red flags needing immediate attention, which is a great start!

Most of the things highlighted in your results fall under "non-compliant validations." These are areas where your data doesn't quite meet a specific standard. Let's go through the key points, focusing on what you can easily improve:

  • Record 'occ5' has several data entry errors:
    • `dwc:basisOfRecord` ("BadBasis"): This value isn't a standard term. You should change "BadBasis" to a recognized Darwin Core `basisOfRecord` like "HumanObservation," "PreservedSpecimen," or "LivingSpecimen."
    • `dwc:country` ("BadCountry"): "BadCountry" is not a known country. Please correct this to a real country name.
    • `dwc:decimalLatitude` (91.0) and `dwc:decimalLongitude` (181.0): These coordinates are outside the possible range for Earth's surface (latitude must be between -90 and 90, longitude between -180 and 180). You'll need to check and correct these location values.

    Correcting these values for 'occ5' will greatly improve the quality and usability of this record, especially for mapping and taxonomic searches.

  • `dwc:occurrenceID` is not a standard URI for any of your records:

    The test checks if your `occurrenceID` values look like standard web addresses (URIs). While your current IDs (`occ1`, `occ2`, etc.) are unique, making them full URIs (e.g., `http://yourdomain.org/occurrence/occ1`) helps make your data more linkable and easier for others to use when it's shared online. This isn't a critical error, but it's a good practice for data sharing.

  • `dwc:scientificName` for most records didn't find an "exact match" in GBIF:

    For species like "Homo sapiens," "Canis lupus," "Felis catus," and "Ursus americanus," the test found the correct scientific name but couldn't make an "exact match" in the GBIF Backbone Taxonomy. This often happens if the author and year (e.g., "Linnaeus, 1758") are missing from your `scientificName` field. Adding the full scientific name with authorship can help achieve an exact match, which improves data integration.

    You might not need to do anything for `occ1` and `occ4` if you're sure your country codes are correct. However, getting your country and country codes consistent is important for global mapping and analysis.
